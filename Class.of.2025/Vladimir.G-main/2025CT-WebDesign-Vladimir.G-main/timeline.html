World War II, which lasted from 1939 to 1945, was a global conflict that involved most of the world's nations, including all of the great powers. It was the most widespread war in history and directly involved more than 100 million people from over 30 countries. The war began on September 1, 1939, when Germany, led by Adolf Hitler, invaded Poland. This act prompted Britain and France to declare war on Germany. The conflict had several root causes, including the Treaty of Versailles' harsh terms imposed on Germany after World War I, economic instability during the Great Depression, and the rise of totalitarian regimes in Germany, Italy, and Japan.

In Europe, Germany quickly conquered much of the continent using a tactic known as Blitzkrieg or "lightning war," which involved rapid, surprise attacks. This led to the swift fall of France and other Western European countries. Britain, however, managed to withstand the sustained air attacks by the German Luftwaffe during the Battle of Britain. The war took a significant turn in 1941 with Operation Barbarossa, Germany's invasion of the Soviet Union. This front became the largest and deadliest theater of war, characterized by immense battles and staggering casualties.

Meanwhile, in the Pacific, the conflict was dominated by the struggle between Japan and the Allies, primarily the United States. The war in the Pacific featured significant naval battles, island-hopping campaigns, and brutal combat conditions. The Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor in December 1941 brought the United States fully into the war, marking a critical turning point. By 1942 and 1943, the Allies began to gain the upper hand with key victories such as the Battle of Midway in the Pacific and the Battle of Stalingrad in the Soviet Union.

The final years of the war saw the Allies making significant advances. In Europe, the D-Day landings on June 6, 1944, marked the beginning of the end for Nazi Germany as Allied forces successfully landed in Normandy, France, and began to push German forces back. By May 1945, Germany had surrendered following the suicide of Adolf Hitler. In the Pacific, the war concluded after the United States dropped atomic bombs on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945, leading to Japan's surrender on September 2, 1945.

The aftermath of World War II had profound and lasting effects on the world. Over 70 million people died, including six million Jews who were murdered in the Holocaust. The war led to significant geopolitical changes, including the establishment of the United Nations to foster international cooperation and prevent future conflicts. The post-war period also saw the emergence of the United States and the Soviet Union as superpowers, setting the stage for the Cold War. Economically, many European and Asian countries were devastated and required extensive rebuilding efforts, such as the Marshall Plan in Europe. World War II reshaped the global order, leading to significant changes in political boundaries, economic systems, and international relations.
